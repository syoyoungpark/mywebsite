Why Do These Tiny Creatures Matter?

Copepods are microscopic crustaceans smaller than a grain of rice, yet they form the backbone of ocean food webs. Pleuromamma species (the focus of this study) perform a daily vertical migration: hiding in the deep ocean during the day to avoid predators, then rising to surface waters at night to feed. This behavior actively pumps carbon from the surface to the deep ocean — making them crucial players in global climate regulation.

How the Research Was Conducted

Egg Production Rates Are Similar Across Species

Despite differences in body size, all four species produce ~20 eggs per female per day

Even though P. xiphias is 2× larger than P. gracilis, both produce the same number of eggs daily. This means smaller species invest proportionally more energy into reproduction.

Bigger Body = Bigger Eggs

A clear positive relationship between female body length and egg diameter (r² = 0.640, p < 0.001)

This pattern mirrors what we see across the animal kingdom — larger animals tend to produce larger offspring.

The Smallest Species Invests the Most in Each Egg

P. gracilis allocates 1.88% of its body carbon per egg — comparable to egg-brooding species

Broadcast spawners (like most copepods) make tiny cheap eggs. Egg-brooders make large, nutrient-rich eggs. P. gracilis sits in egg-brooding territory — unusual for a species that freely spawns eggs into the water.

"P. gracilis — the smallest species — migrates the shortest distance, feeds near the food-rich surface, and channels that extra energy directly into producing higher-quality eggs."

P. xiphias

Largest body · Deepest migration (>600m) · Lowest egg carbon investment (15% of body carbon per clutch)

P. abdominalis

Medium body · Mid-depth migration (200–600m) · Medium investment (27%)

P. gracilis 🏆

Smallest body · Shallow migration (<100m) · Highest investment (43% of body carbon per clutch)
